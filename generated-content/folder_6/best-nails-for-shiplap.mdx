## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Nails For Shiplap

Choosing the right materials is the foundation of a successful shiplap project. This guide will walk you through the key considerations for selecting both the shiplap planks and the nails to ensure a beautiful, long-lasting result.

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
First, define your project's aesthetic. Are you aiming for a rustic, modern farmhouse, or coastal look? This will guide your choice of shiplap. Reclaimed wood like that from BARNWOODUSA offers authentic, weathered character, while new, pre-primed planks provide a cleaner, more uniform look. Then, consider the nails. For a seamless finish, small-headed finishing or brad nails (like those in the Mr. Pen kit) are ideal as they can be hidden. If you want the nails to be a design element, opt for decorative wrought head nails like those from MOLIGOU.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations
Your budget will be split between planks and nails. Shiplap planks can range widely, from approximately $4 to $15 per square foot, depending on whether it's new pine, MDF, or authentic reclaimed barn wood. Nails are a much smaller part of the cost. A versatile kit like the Mr. Pen assortment can cost between $10-$15 for 600 nails. Basic box nails from Hillman are even more affordable. Specialty decorative nails can cost $10-$20 for a small pack of 20, as you're paying for the craftsmanship.

### Key Features - Most important technical specifications
For shiplap planks, look at:
*   **Material:** Real wood (pine, cedar, reclaimed) vs. MDF. Real wood offers authenticity, while MDF is often cheaper and more uniform.
*   **Thickness & Width:** Thicker planks (around 1/2-inch) are more durable. Width affects the visual style; wider planks look more modern.
*   **Coverage:** Always buy 10-15% more square footage than you measure to account for cuts and mistakes.

For nails, consider:
*   **Gauge (Thickness):** 16 or 18-gauge nails are a good standard for shiplap. They are thin enough to reduce splitting but strong enough to hold.
*   **Length:** The nail should be long enough to pass through the shiplap, the drywall, and at least 3/4-inch into the wall stud. A 2-inch nail is a safe bet for most applications.
*   **Head Type:** Brad nails have a very small head for hiding. Finishing nails have a slightly larger head for more holding power. Decorative nails have large, stylized heads.

### Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction
High-quality shiplap planks will be straight, with minimal warping, and consistent milling for easy interlocking. For reclaimed wood, look for planks that have been properly cleaned and treated. For nails, quality means they won't bend easily when hammered. A galvanized or coated finish provides corrosion resistance, which is important if you're installing shiplap in a potentially damp area like a bathroom or mudroom.

### Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers
Sticking with reputable brands can save you headaches. Brands like BARNWOODUSA and Luxdecor are known for their quality reclaimed wood products. For fasteners, names like Hillman have been trusted for decades to produce consistent, reliable nails. Emerging brands like Mr. Pen and MOLIGOU have quickly built a reputation for value and specialty designs, respectively. Reading user reviews can provide insight into a brand's quality control and customer satisfaction.

### Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage
For nails, a well-organized case like the one from Mr. Pen is invaluable. It keeps your supplies tidy and easy to transport to your workspace. For shiplap, consider the logistics of getting the boxes into your home. Planks often come in lengths up to 8 feet or longer. Ensure you have a clear path and a safe, dry place to store them before installation, allowing them to acclimate to your home's humidity for a few days to prevent future warping.

## What Is the Best Way to Choose nails for shiplap?

Choosing the best nails and planks for your shiplap project can be straightforward if you follow a few simple steps. This methodical approach ensures you get the right materials for a flawless, lasting finish.

1.  **Measure and Plan:** Before anything else, accurately measure the height and width of the wall you intend to cover to calculate the total square footage. Add 10-15% to this number for waste from cuts. Decide on the orientation of your planks—horizontal, vertical, or herringbone—as this can affect your measurements and the final look.

2.  **Select Your Shiplap:** Based on your budget and desired aesthetic, choose your shiplap material. For an authentic, rustic feel, opt for reclaimed wood planks. For a clean, paintable surface, pre-primed MDF or pine is an excellent choice. Pay attention to the thickness of the planks, as this will influence your nail choice.

3.  **Choose the Right Nail Type:**
    *   **For a Hidden Finish:** If you're using a nail gun, 18-gauge brad nails are perfect. They are thin, leave a tiny hole, and are strong enough for most shiplap. If hammering by hand, use a slender finishing nail and a nail set to sink the head just below the wood surface.
    *   **For a Decorative Finish:** If you want the fasteners to be part of the design, choose wrought head or clavos nails. These are meant to be visible and add a rustic, handcrafted touch.

4.  **Determine the Correct Nail Length:** This is crucial. Your nail must penetrate the shiplap, the drywall (typically 1/2-inch), and embed at least 3/4-inch into the wooden stud behind it. For standard 1/2-inch shiplap, a 2-inch nail (6D) is the most common and effective length.

5.  **Avoid Common Mistakes:** A frequent error is using nails that are too short, which won't hold securely, or too thick, which can split the shiplap tongue. Another mistake is nailing only into the drywall. Always use a stud finder to locate and mark your studs, ensuring every nail has a solid anchor.

## FAQ

### What is the best nails for shiplap for beginners?
For beginners, the best option is an 18-gauge brad nail used with a pneumatic nail gun, as it's fast and leaves a very small, easy-to-fill hole. If you're hammering by hand, a versatile kit like the Mr. Pen Nail Assortment is fantastic. It provides multiple sizes, and the slender finishing nails are less likely to split the wood than thicker common nails.

### How much should I spend on nails for shiplap?
Nails are a very small portion of your total project cost. You can expect to spend between $10 and $25 for more than enough nails for an entire accent wall. A large, versatile kit might cost around $15, while a small box of specific finishing nails could be under $10. Decorative nails are more expensive, potentially costing $1 per nail.

### What features are most important in nails for shiplap?
The three most important features are length, gauge (thickness), and head type. The length must be sufficient to securely anchor in the wall studs (typically 2 inches). The gauge should be thin enough to prevent splitting the wood (16 or 18-gauge is ideal). The head should be small for a hidden finish or intentionally decorative for a rustic look.

### How do I maintain my nails for shiplap?
Once installed and the holes are filled and painted, the nails themselves require no maintenance. They are protected within the wall. For exposed decorative nails, a simple dusting now and then is all that's needed. If you have leftover nails, store them in a dry place to prevent rust, preferably in their original, organized container.

### Are expensive nails for shiplap worth it?
It depends on the goal. For a standard, painted shiplap wall where nails will be hidden, expensive nails are not necessary; a reliable, affordable finishing nail is perfect. However, if you are creating a rustic feature where the nails are visible, investing in decorative wrought head nails is absolutely worth it as they become a key part of the design.

### What brands make the best nails for shiplap?
For general-purpose nails, trusted brands like Hillman are known for their consistency and quality. For value-packed assortment kits, Mr. Pen offers an excellent and highly-rated option. For specialty decorative nails that provide a rustic, hand-forged look, MOLIGOU is a standout choice that adds significant character to a project.

### How long do nails for shiplap typically last?
When installed correctly in an interior wall, the nails and the shiplap itself will last for decades—essentially the lifetime of the wall. The nails are not exposed to elements that would cause them to corrode or fail. The durability of your shiplap wall is determined by the wood and the quality of the installation, not the lifespan of the nails.

### Can I find good nails for shiplap on a budget?
Yes, absolutely. You do not need to spend a lot of money to get high-quality fasteners. A product like the Hillman Polished Box Nails provides excellent performance at a very low cost. The key is to ensure they are the correct length and gauge for your specific shiplap planks, not how much they cost.

### What's the difference between brad nails and finishing nails for shiplap?
Brad nails (usually 18-gauge) are thinner and have a much smaller head than finishing nails (usually 15 or 16-gauge). Brads are great for attaching thin shiplap and leave a tiny hole that often doesn't need filling. Finishing nails offer slightly more holding power due to their larger size, making them a good choice for thicker or heavier wood planks.

### Do I need a nail gun to install shiplap?
A nail gun is not strictly necessary, but it makes the job significantly faster and easier. You can absolutely install shiplap with a traditional hammer and finishing nails. If you do, it's highly recommended to use a nail set to sink the nail heads slightly below the wood's surface without denting the plank with the hammer.
