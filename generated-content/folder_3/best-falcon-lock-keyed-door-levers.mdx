## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Falcon Lock Keyed Door Levers

Choosing the right door lever involves more than just picking a style. It's a decision that impacts your property's security, accessibility, and daily convenience. To make an informed choice, consider these critical factors before you buy.

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
First, determine the lever's function. Do you need a keyed entry for an office, a privacy lever for a restroom, a storeroom lever that's always locked from the outside, or a simple passage lever for a hallway? Also, check your door's specifications. Measure the backset (the distance from the edge of the door to the center of the bore hole), which is typically 2-3/8" or 2-3/4". Confirm your door's thickness (usually 1-3/8" to 1-3/4") to ensure a proper fit. For commercial properties, ensure the lever is ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) compliant for accessibility.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations ($X-$Y ranges)
Commercial-grade door levers vary in price. Light-duty commercial models can range from $30 to $60. For ANSI Grade 2 medium-to-heavy-duty levers, expect to pay between $60 and $120, depending on the brand, function, and finish. Premium finishes like polished brass or oil-rubbed bronze may be at the higher end of this range. While budget options are tempting, investing in a certified, durable lever often saves money in the long run by avoiding premature replacement.

### Key Features - Most important technical specifications
Look for key technical specs that indicate quality. An ANSI/BHMA Grade 2 certification is the standard for most commercial applications, signifying the lock has passed rigorous tests for cycles, strength, and security. A UL fire rating (e.g., 3-Hour) is crucial for doors in fire-rated walls. Consider the keyway type, like the common Schlage C, which allows for easier re-keying and integration into a master key system. Finally, features like a deadlocking latch bolt provide extra security against tampering.

### Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction
The materials used are a direct indicator of durability. Look for levers made from solid zinc, brass, or stainless steel. Internal components made of cold-rolled steel are a sign of a heavy-duty build. A solid chassis with thru-bolts that go through the door will prevent the lever from loosening or sagging over time, a common issue with levers that only use two screws.

### Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers
Brands like Falcon, Dynasty Hardware, Lawrence, and MrArm have established reputations for producing reliable commercial hardware. These manufacturers adhere to strict industry standards and often back their products with substantial warranties (from several years to a decade). Choosing a reputable brand ensures you're getting a product that has been tested and proven in real-world applications, and you'll have better access to customer support if needed.

### Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage
While portability isn't a factor for an installed lever, consider the lever's projection and design. Ensure the lever won't interfere with walls or other objects when the door is opened. The size of the rosette (the decorative plate behind the lever) should be large enough to cover any marks or paint rings from previous hardware. A non-handed or reversible design is a significant advantage, as it simplifies ordering and installation by fitting both left- and right-swinging doors.

## What Is the Best Way to Choose falcon lock keyed door levers?

Choosing the best falcon lock keyed door levers requires a methodical approach to ensure you get the right hardware for your specific needs. Follow this step-by-step guide for the best results.

**1. Assess the Application:**
Start by defining the door's purpose. Is it an exterior entry door, an office, a secure storeroom, a private restroom, or a simple hallway? This will determine the required function: Entry, Storeroom, Privacy, or Passage.

**2. Verify Door Specifications:**
Before you shop, measure your door. You need to know the thickness, the backset (distance from door edge to the center of the lock hole), and the bore hole diameter. The standard for commercial doors is a 2-3/4" backset and a 2-1/8" bore, but always verify.

**3. Prioritize Certification:**
For any commercial or high-traffic use, look for an ANSI/BHMA Grade 2 certification at a minimum. This ensures the lock is built for durability and security. If the door is part of a fire barrier, a UL 3-Hour fire rating is non-negotiable. Also, ensure the lever is ADA compliant for public accessibility.

**4. Examine Construction and Materials:**
Look beyond the finish. Check for details like a solid (not hollow) lever, stainless steel latch bolts, and thru-bolt mounting capability. These features indicate a higher-quality build that will resist wear and sagging.

**5. Consider Keying Needs:**
Do you need the new lock to work with existing keys? If so, ensure it has a compatible keyway (like the common Schlage C) that a locksmith can re-key. If you're keying multiple doors, consider options for keyed-alike sets.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
*   **Buying Residential Grade for Commercial Use:** Residential levers are not built to withstand the high frequency of use in a commercial setting and will fail prematurely.
*   **Ignoring Door Prep:** Buying a lever with the wrong backset or that doesn't fit your door's bore hole will lead to installation headaches.
*   **Forgetting about Handing:** While many modern levers are non-handed (reversible), some are not. Always check if you need a specific right-hand or left-hand lever.

**When to Upgrade vs. Budget Options:**
Opt for a premium, heavy-duty model (like those with Grade 2 certification and robust steel components) for main entry points, high-security areas, and extremely high-traffic doors. A budget-friendly, light-duty commercial model can be a suitable choice for interior office doors or less-frequented areas where ADA compliance and a professional look are still needed, but the demand for durability is lower.
