## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Intermediate Classical Guitar

While we've focused on essential books and accessories, choosing the right instrument is paramount. Here’s a guide to selecting the best intermediate classical guitar itself.

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
Consider what your goals are. Are you a dedicated student aiming for conservatory auditions, or a passionate hobbyist who wants a beautiful sound for personal enjoyment? If you plan to perform, look for a guitar with good projection and dynamic range. If you're primarily a recording artist, focus on tonal balance and clarity. Your personal goals will dictate the specific features you need.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations
An intermediate classical guitar is a significant step up from a beginner model. Expect to spend in the range of $400 to $1,500 for a quality instrument.
- **$400 - $700:** You can find excellent guitars with solid tops (usually cedar or spruce) and laminate back and sides. Brands like Cordoba (C5, C7) and Yamaha (CG series) offer fantastic value here.
- **$700 - $1,500:** In this range, you'll find all-solid wood guitars, which produce a richer, more complex tone that improves with age. You'll also see better craftsmanship, higher-quality tuners, and features like ebony fretboards.

### Key Features - Most important technical specifications
- **Top Wood:** The top is the most critical element for tone. **Cedar** tops offer a warm, rich, and open sound from the beginning. **Spruce** tops are typically brighter, clearer, and take longer to 'open up', but can develop more complexity over time.
- **Solid vs. Laminate:** A **solid top** is essential for an intermediate guitar. It vibrates more freely than a laminate (plywood) top, resulting in better volume, sustain, and tonal richness. All-solid wood guitars (solid top, back, and sides) offer the best possible tone.
- **Neck Profile and Nut Width:** Classical guitars have wider necks than steel-string acoustics. The standard nut width is 52mm (about 2 inches), but some models offer slightly narrower "crossover" necks. Try a few to see what feels most comfortable for your hands.
- **Action:** This is the height of the strings off the fretboard. The action should be low enough for comfortable playing but high enough to avoid buzzing. This can be adjusted by a professional luthier.

### Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction
Inspect the guitar's craftsmanship. Look for clean glue joints inside the body, a well-seated bridge, and smooth fret ends. The finish should be even and free of major blemishes. High-quality tuning machines are also important; they should turn smoothly and hold the pitch well. A truss rod in the neck (common in many modern intermediate models) is a huge plus, as it allows for adjustments to keep the neck straight.

### Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers
Sticking with reputable brands like **Cordoba, Yamaha, Kremona, or Alhambra** is a safe bet. These companies have a long history of consistent quality control and good customer support. While boutique or luthier-built guitars can be amazing, an established brand often provides better value and reliability in the intermediate price range.

### Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage
Most classical guitars are full-size (4/4). However, if you are a smaller person, you might consider a 7/8 scale model, which can be more comfortable to play without a significant sacrifice in tone. Always invest in a good-quality hard case or a heavily padded gig bag to protect your instrument during transport and storage, especially if it's an all-solid wood guitar that is more sensitive to changes in humidity and temperature.

## Is it A Wise Decision to Buy intermediate classical guitar?

Investing in a high-quality intermediate classical guitar is one of the most impactful decisions you can make in your musical journey. While it may seem like a significant financial step up from a beginner instrument, the benefits are immense and justify the cost. An intermediate guitar is not just an expense; it's an investment in your skill, passion, and enjoyment.

The primary difference lies in the materials, specifically the use of a solid wood top. This single feature dramatically enhances the instrument's tone, providing a richness, volume, and complexity that a laminate beginner guitar simply cannot produce. This improved sound is incredibly inspiring. It makes your practice sessions more rewarding and allows you to explore musical nuances like dynamics and tone color more effectively. You'll find that the music you play sounds closer to how it's meant to sound, which is a powerful motivator.

Compared to the alternative of struggling with a beginner guitar that limits your expression, an intermediate model unlocks your potential. The better playability, including a more comfortable neck and better action, reduces physical strain and allows you to tackle more demanding pieces. In the long term, a quality intermediate guitar will serve you for many years, and many even improve in tone as the solid wood ages. It's a wise, long-term investment that will pay dividends in your growth and satisfaction as a musician.

## What Is the Best Way to Choose intermediate classical guitar?

Choosing your intermediate classical guitar is a personal process. Following a structured approach can help you find the perfect instrument for your needs.

1.  **Define Your Budget:** First, establish a realistic price range. Knowing whether you are shopping in the $500 or $1,200 bracket will narrow down your options significantly.
2.  **Research Tonewoods:** Decide between a cedar top (warm, immediate tone) and a spruce top (bright, developing tone). Listen to online comparisons to understand the difference. There is no right answer; it's purely a matter of personal preference.
3.  **Play Before You Buy:** This is the most critical step. Visit music stores and play as many different guitars in your price range as you can. Pay attention to how the neck feels in your hand, how comfortable the body is to hold, and, most importantly, how it sounds to you.
4.  **Check for Playability:** Assess the string height (action). Is it comfortable to press the strings down? Does the guitar have any buzzing sounds when you play on different frets? A good store will have their guitars set up properly.
5.  **Inspect the Craftsmanship:** Look over the guitar carefully. Check for any flaws in the finish, sharp fret ends, or a bridge that is lifting off the top. A well-made instrument is a sign of quality.
6.  **Avoid Common Mistakes:** Don't buy a guitar based solely on brand name or online reviews. Your personal experience playing the instrument is what matters most. Also, don't overlook the importance of a professional setup. Even a great guitar can play poorly if it's not adjusted correctly. A luthier can adjust the action and intonation to make it perfect for you.
7.  **Budget vs. Upgrade:** A solid-top guitar is a must. If your budget allows, upgrading to an all-solid wood guitar offers the best long-term tonal benefits. However, a well-made solid-top/laminate-back guitar from a reputable brand can be an outstanding and satisfying instrument for years.

## FAQ

### What is the best intermediate classical guitar for beginners?
For a beginner transitioning to intermediate, a guitar like the Cordoba C7 is an excellent choice. It features a solid wood top (available in cedar or spruce), which provides a significant upgrade in tone. It's well-built, comfortable to play, and offers fantastic value, making the step up feel meaningful without being overwhelmingly expensive.

### How much should I spend on intermediate classical guitar?
A good budget for a quality intermediate classical guitar is between $400 and $1,500. In the lower end of this range ($400-$700), you'll find excellent solid-top models. In the upper end ($700-$1,500), you can get all-solid wood instruments that offer superior tone and will age beautifully over time.

### What features are most important in intermediate classical guitar?
The single most important feature is a solid wood top (either cedar or spruce). This is what primarily determines the quality of the guitar's tone. Other important features include quality tuning machines that hold pitch well, a comfortable neck profile, and good overall craftsmanship. A truss rod is also a valuable feature for long-term neck stability.

### How do I maintain my intermediate classical guitar?
Keep your guitar in a case with a humidifier, especially if it's an all-solid wood model and you live in a dry climate. This prevents the wood from cracking. Change your strings regularly (every 1-3 months for active players) to maintain a bright, clear tone. Wipe down the strings and body with a soft cloth after playing to remove oils and dirt.

### Are expensive intermediate classical guitar worth it?
Yes, to a point. Moving from a $200 beginner guitar to a $600 intermediate model offers a massive improvement in sound and playability. The jump from a $600 to a $1,200 all-solid model offers another noticeable, though perhaps more subtle, improvement. The extra cost typically gets you better materials (all-solid woods, ebony fretboards) and finer craftsmanship, resulting in a more resonant and responsive instrument.

### What brands make the best intermediate classical guitar?
Several brands are highly respected for their intermediate classical guitars. Cordoba is arguably the market leader, offering a wide range of excellent models. Yamaha is known for its incredible consistency and quality control. Other fantastic brands to look for include Kremona, Alhambra, and La Patrie.

### How long do intermediate classical guitar typically last?
A well-made intermediate classical guitar, if properly cared for, can last a lifetime. Solid wood instruments can actually sound better as they age, a process known as 'opening up'. Regular maintenance, proper humidification, and avoiding extreme temperatures are key to ensuring its longevity.

### Can I find good intermediate classical guitar on a budget?
Absolutely. The $400-$600 price range is a sweet spot for value. You can find guitars from brands like Yamaha and Cordoba that feature solid tops and excellent playability. While they may have laminate back and sides to keep costs down, they still represent a huge leap in quality from a typical beginner instrument and are more than capable of producing a beautiful sound.
